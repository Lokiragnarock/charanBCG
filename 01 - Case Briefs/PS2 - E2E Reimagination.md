# PS2 — Reimagine an Everyday Product

> **CHOSEN CASE** — decided 2026-07-09, see [[../04 - Decision Log/2026-07-09 - Vault opened|decision log]].

## The task (one paragraph)
Pick **one** everyday household product, consumer appliance, or personal electronic device that has **barely changed in ~20 years**. Reimagine it **end-to-end** — the product, the experience around it, and the business behind it — **for the world of 2030**. "End-to-end" = past the object: features & form, UI/UX & experience, who desires it & why, buildability, cost, supply-chain impact, go-to-market.

## ⚠️ The "Why Now" gate (the case's kill-switch)
The reimagination must clear one bar: **why is this possible NOW and not 5–10 years ago?**
- Name the *specific unlock*: a technology that got cheap/good enough, a changed behaviour, a new material, a regulation, a cost curve.
- "Could have been built in 2010 but wasn't" = a product that failed to launch, **not a reinvention**. Instant disqualifier.

## The four lenses (take a POV, not a list of options)
| Lens | Questions |
|---|---|
| **Desirability** | Who is it for? What job does it do better? Why switch? Form, UI/UX, feel? |
| **Feasibility** | Buildable with what exists (or will) by 2030? Components, materials, capabilities? Supply-chain impact? |
| **Viability** | Cost to make & buy? Revenue model (one-off / subscription / service / platform)? GTM? |
| **Scalability** | *(listed but left blank in the brief — likely intentional or an editing gap; we should define and answer it anyway: unit economics at volume, manufacturing ramp, channel scale)* |

## Evidence expected (all cited)
Bill-of-materials estimates, component cost-curve trends, category market sizes, adoption data for comparable products, patents, **user reviews revealing what people hate about the current product**.

## What makes a winning answer here
- One product, one sharp POV — the brief explicitly punishes optionality
- A named, dated, quantified "why now" unlock (cost curve with numbers)
- Real BOM math, not vibes
- Evidence of hate for the incumbent (reviews = free user research)
